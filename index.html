In over a decade of financial crime investigation — tracing terrorism financing networks, mapping criminal organisations across jurisdictions, and building AI investigative platforms — the most critical skill I have developed is not knowing the answer. It is knowing how to formulate the right question.

AI does not change that truth. It magnifies it.

"Garbage in, garbage out" is not a cliché. In intelligence work, we call it the collection gap. The analyst who asks a vague question gets vague intelligence — and makes decisions on it.

After leading 450+ cases and training 200+ officers across Mena Region and Europe I have seen the same failure repeat in human investigations and AI interactions alike: people rush to the answer before they understand the question.

The Five Rules

The Ask Like an Analyst framework — built in the field, applicable everywhere.

The "Ask Like an Analyst" Method
Rule 01

Define your role and goal first.

An AI with no context is like a new recruit on day one — willing, but lost. Tell it who you are and what decision you need to make. Context transforms the answer.

Rule 02

Name the format you need.

A well-structured intelligence report is worth ten walls of raw data. Bullet points, a draft email, a comparison table — structure your ask to match what you'll actually use.

Rule 03

Set the constraint.

We never ask "tell me everything." We ask a specific question with a defined scope. Constraints are not limitations — they are precision. "Two sentences for a non-specialist" beats "summarise this" every time.

Rule 04

Give it a target, not a topic.

A topic is "financial crime." A target is "explain to a small business owner why their bank might ask for enhanced due diligence." Targets produce actionable answers. Topics produce encyclopaedia entries.

Rule 05

Iterate like a conversation, not a search engine.

The first response is a draft, not a conclusion. Follow up. Push back. Ask for alternatives. The best analysts never accepted the first read — they interrogated it. Do the same with AI.

The mistake that can cost you your job
Rule 6: Never confuse context with data.
Context is your role. Data is your client's name. AI needs the first. It must never receive the second.

In intelligence and financial crime investigation, we call this operational security. When I built UNECTOOLS — my AI-powered investigative platform — its entire architecture is built on a zero-knowledge principle: the platform itself cannot access the data investigators upload. That decision was not accidental. It was a direct response to what happens when sensitive data meets an external system without controls.

The same risk exists every time you open a public AI tool at work. Most people don't think twice before pasting in a client email, a contract, or a financial report. They focus on getting an answer — not protecting the data behind it. But what they share may be stored, used to train future models, or reviewed by human operators in ways they never consented to.

Never share with public AI
Real client names · company financials · internal case files · employee data · contract terms · anything marked confidential.
Always use instead
Your role · a generic situation description · anonymised scenarios · hypothetical examples that carry the structure of your problem — without the sensitive content.

Give AI enough to understand your situation.
Never enough to identify anyone in it.
